Engagement Without Retention: A Live-Service Case Study

Overview

A video game publisher was losing ~6% of its live-service game's subscriber base each quarter despite regular major content updates. The goal: examine user behavior before and after the latest update and identify the driving force behind the dropping numbers.

Problem Statement

Subscriptions were in decline, but social listening was proving insufficient to identify the contributing factors. The business needed to measure player engagement and determine why the content updates were not improving retention.

Methodology

The project followed a standard analysis procedure with emphasis on interpretability (the product team needed to understand why subscriptions were dropping, not just that they were):

  • Extracted 4 months of game session data from the database via SQL
  • Used Python to clean up data quality issues, including missing values, incorrect data types, and inconsistent formatting
  • Engineered a metric to measure player engagement using weighted measures of login frequency, session duration, and participation in social activities to better compare engagement trends across player cohorts
  • Added calculated fields to the dataset via Python to track users' engagement scores
  • Visualized the data in Tableau and compiled relevant views into a dashboard that told the data's story
[ carousel of Jupyter Notebook images ]

Key Findings

The data revealed two major trends:

  • The wealth of new content drove up play time across every player cohort (+25.07% minutes per session)
  • Players continued to log in less over time (-10.73% daily active users), with casual players seeing the biggest drop-off

The latest patch provided plenty of content for players to engage with once but lacked sufficient repeatable content that players could return to again and again. The existing repeatable content was least appealing to casual players.

Recommendations

Future content updates need to provide players with repeatable content that will keep them coming back for more. Repeatable content needs to be carefully balanced to keep hardcore players happy while being accessible and engaging for casual players.

  • Survey the community to determine what each player cohort wants to see in repeatable content
  • Build one new game system that will bring repeatable content with something for every player type to future updates
  • Run an open playtest of the new system; target casual players in testing groups

Tools & Technologies

Python (Pandas, NumPy, Matplotlib, Seaborn), SQL, Tableau
